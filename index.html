On the Beach

Maya is at the beach, soaking up the sun. The warmth feels strong, and soon her skin stings and a rash appears across her cheeks. She feels unusually tired.
"I love the beach, but this sun is too much. Why am I so tired? Why does my skin feel this way?"
Let’s pause: If Maya was your friend, what would you gently suggest next?



At Home

Back home, Maya tries to do chores. Her arms and hands ache, her energy vanishes quickly, and rest doesn’t help.
"Washing a few dishes shouldn’t wear me out like this. My wrists and fingers feel stiff and sore. What’s wrong with me?"
For discussion: If Maya told you about her constant tiredness and pain, how would you advise her?



At Work

At work, Maya drinks coffee but still feels exhausted. The rash is visible, her joints ache, and it's getting harder to focus.
"I keep falling behind. I wish people would understand—this isn’t just being tired. Something deeper is going on."
Imagine you're checking in with Maya: Which of these symptoms suggests she should seek further medical advice?



Emergency Room

Maya’s pain and fatigue become overwhelming. Feeling scared, she heads to the ER for help, hoping someone will listen and take her concerns seriously.
"It’s hard to explain everything I’m feeling. I’m worried no one will believe me, but I can’t ignore these symptoms anymore."
In the role of a triage nurse: What response would best help Maya feel heard and safe?



Admitted to Hospital

After triage, Maya is admitted for further evaluation. She’s unsure what’s happening and feels frightened by the uncertainty.
"I’m alone in this bed. Nurses and doctors come and go, asking me questions. I wish I understood what they’re looking for."
Let’s reflect: If you were a medical student seeing Maya, what could you do to help her feel more comfortable?



Reflection: As a future physician, what words, gestures, or approaches might help Maya feel seen and supported?

Doctor – Patient Conversation

Doctor: Hello Maya, thank you for coming in today. I understand you’ve been feeling very tired and have some joint pain?
Maya: Yes, I’m just so exhausted all the time and my hands and wrists hurt. I also have this rash that won’t go away.
Doctor: That sounds really tough. How long have you been experiencing these symptoms?
Maya: It started a few weeks ago, but it keeps getting worse. Some days I can barely do anything.
Doctor: You’re not alone, and I want you to know that we take these symptoms seriously. Can you tell me if anything makes them better or worse?
Maya: Rest sometimes helps, but everything seems harder lately. Being in the sun makes the rash worse.
Doctor: Thank you for sharing that. Our next step will be to run some tests to better understand what’s going on. While we wait for those results, is there anything you’re most worried about right now?
Maya: I’m worried no one will find out what’s wrong. I just want to feel normal again.
Doctor: I understand how frustrating it is not to have answers. We’ll work together to figure this out, and I’ll keep you updated every step of the way. If you feel worse or have new symptoms, please let us know right away. And if you have questions or concerns at any point, I’m here to listen.
Reflect: What parts of this conversation show empathy? If you were in the doctor's position, what would you add or change?

Testing and Waiting

Bloodwork and other tests are ordered. Maya answers countless questions and waits. No one gives her clear answers yet.
"The waiting is hard—I'm anxious, and I just want someone to explain things to me. Every test makes me nervous."
Thinking ahead: What emotions is Maya most likely experiencing during this uncertain wait?



Reflect: What could reassure Maya at this moment? If you were on her care team, how would you approach these conversations?

Hospital Progression

Maya speaks with a doctor about her lab results and symptoms. She’s told that more information is needed and that her team will keep her informed. She still doesn’t have a name for her illness.
"I feel lost and scared. I’m grateful they’re trying to help, but I wish I knew what’s going on inside my body."
Reflect: Why is patient communication important at this stage? What questions would you encourage Maya to ask?

Next Steps

Maya’s care team continues to investigate her symptoms. She hopes for answers and appreciates clinicians who listen and explain.
"Every kind word and clear explanation helps. Even when there aren't answers yet, knowing someone cares makes a difference."
Reflect: If you were Maya’s clinician, what would you do next, and how would you communicate this to her?
